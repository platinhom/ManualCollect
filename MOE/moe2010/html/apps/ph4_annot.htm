Introduction

A pharmacophore annotation scheme, or more simply a scheme, is an automatic procedure to assign pharmacophore annotation points (such as H-bond donor, H-bond acceptor, hydrophobe, etc.) to a 3D conformation of a molecule. The pharmacophore annotation points are not part of the molecule proper, even though some of the annotation points may be located directly on top of a particular atom of the molecule. The annotation points are what are examined by the MOE pharmacophore search; in other words, the annotation points are what the pharmacophore search "sees" when considering a particular molecular conformation. In contrast, a pharmacophore query is a collection of query features forming a (possibly complex) pattern that may or may not match a particular set of annotation points of a conformation. MOE has a variety of built-in schemes which provide different types of annotations and use different policies to locate the annotations about a particular conformation. Custom schemes can also be implemented in SVL.

Annotation points divide into three broad categories:

  1. Atom annotations are located directly on an atom of a molecule, such as "H-bond donor" or "cation" and typically indicate a function related to protein-ligand binding.
  2. Centroid Annotations are located at the geometric center of a subset of the atoms of a molecule. For example, an aromatic ring annotation will typically be located at the centroid of the atoms of the ring. Centroid annotations can also be used to indicate hydrophobic regions.
  3. Projected Annotations are (typically) located along implicit lone pair directions or implicit hydrogen directions and are used to annotate the location of possible hydrogen bond partners, metal ligation partners, or possible R-group atom locations.

In the remainder of this document, the particulars of the MOE pharmacophore schemes will be described along with the methods used to determine the locations of the annotations supported by each scheme.

The Unified is the most comprehensive and includes all of the annotation types of the other schemes. The other schemes are provided largely for compatibility with MOE versions prior to 2007 which, for reasons of efficiency, provided schemes with subsets of the total annotation collection. In the 2007 version of MOE, the pharmacophore system achieves the required efficiency with the Unified scheme despite its large collection of annotation types.

Note! While the Unified scheme contains the annotation types of all of the other schemes, its typing rules and geometry assignments differ from the other schemes which largely share the same rules. Some feature types, such as AccP, have been replaced by other feature types in the Unified scheme, but are still available in other schemes. We mark these feature types as obsolete.

Warning! The annotation schemes assume that the input conformations represent exact (washed) structures; in other words, carboxylate is considered different from carboxylic acid and no implicit tautomer transformations are performed. Molecules are annotated as-presented.

H-Bond Donors/Acceptors and Metal Ligators

The hydrogen bonding and metal ligation annotations consist of the following:

  1. Don annotates an H-bond donor heavy atom.
  2. Don2 annotates projected locations of potential H-bond acceptors.
  3. Acc annotates an H-bond acceptor heavy atom.
  4. Acc2 annotates projected locations of potential H-bond donors.
  5. ML annotates a metal ligator heavy atom.
  6. ML2 annotates projected locations of potential ligated metals.
  7. DonP annotates an sp or sp2 H-bond donor heavy atom (obsolete).
  8. DonS annotates an sp3 H-bond donor heavy atom (obsolete).
  9. AccP annotates an sp or sp2 H-bond acceptor heavy atom (obsolete).
  10. AccS annotates an sp3 H-bond acceptor heavy atom (obsolete).

A Don annotation is added to all oxygen and nitrogen atoms with at least one (possibly implicit) hydrogen attached. Projected Don2 annotations are added according to the hybridization and the heavy atom coordination of the donor. In the following table the d denotes a Don2 feature.

Q = 1° sp3, X >1-coord, Y ≠ H Q = 2° sp3 Q = 3° sp3
Q = 1° sp2 Q = 1° sp2, X ≠ H Q = 2° sp2
 
  Q : 2° sp2 Q = 1° sp  

Hydrogen bond Acc2 projected annotations are added to those heavy atoms that qualify as H-bond acceptors (see below) and are given Acc annotations. The Acc2 projected annotations are added in the same locations as those for the Don2 projected annotations and according to the same rules. (That is, the donors and the acceptors are projected using the same angles and the same distances.) An atom that is both a Don and an Acc will be annotated with "Don2&Acc2" projected annotations.

Only {O,S,N} elements can be hydrogen bond acceptors provided that they conform to the following rules:

  1. Sulfur atoms are not acceptors except that sulfur in S=C groups and anionic sulfurs are acceptors and are given Acc annotations.
  2. Nitrogen atoms are acceptors and given Acc annotations provided that they are not buried. A buried nitrogen is one of {=N=, −N#, >N=, >N<} or a non-3-ring conjugated nitrogen of the form {>N−π, >N−[C+], >N−B, >N−S=O, >N−P=O}.
  3. Oxygen atoms are acceptors and are given Acc annotations provided that they are not buried and provided that they are not in certain exception groups. A buried oxygen is one of {=O=, −O#; >O=, >O<} or a non-3-ring conjugated oxygen of the form {>O−π, >O−B, >O−[C+]}. Non-buried oxygen atoms are acceptors unless they are part of the following exception groups:

not Acc; Xi = any
not Acc if any Xi = het; Y ≠ H

not Acc if Y ≠ H, E = {C#N, N#C, >[N,S,P]=O, Q+, CF3}
not Acc if both {Q1,Q2} are π
not Acc if both {X1,X2} = het

The metal ligand annotation ML is added to the heavy atom of all deemed metal ligands. Anionic atoms and H-bond acceptor atoms are always given ML annotations as well as certain specific groups such as phosphines and mercaptans. Projected annotations ML2 are added in the same positions and according to the same rules as the Don2 projected annotations.

The DonP and DonS annotations are unique to the PPCH and PPCH_All schemes and further distinguish π from non-π donors (respectively). The AccP and AccS annotations are unique to the PPCH and PPCH_All schemes and further distinguish π from non-π acceptors (respectively). The DonP, DonS, AccP and AccS are retained for compatibility. The Unified scheme achieves these distinctions with use of the pi annotation. This annotation is used to indicate that any of Acc, Acc2, Don, Don2, ML or ML2 belong to an atom in a π system. Thus DonP is equivalent to "Don&pi" and DonS can be specified in a query with the expression "Don&!pi".

Ionic Groups

The following annotations are used for ionic atoms and groups:

  1. Ani annotations are used on anionic heavy atoms.
  2. Cat annotations are used on cationic heavy atoms.
  3. O2 annotations are used for bioisosteres of −CO2.
  4. CN2 annotations are used for bioisosteres of −CN2.

The Ani annotation is assigned to heavy atoms with an excess of negative charge, including excess charge due to resonances (of certain groups). An atom with a formal negative charge not adjacent to an atom with a formal positive charge will be assigned an Ani annotation. Both oxygen atoms of [O−]−C=C−C=O are also marked with Ani. Up to three anionic resonances between [N−,S−,O−] and [=N,=S,=O] are taken into account in anionic carboxylates, (acyl) sulfonamides, muscimol, anionic imidazole, tetrazole etc, and up to four anionic {O,S} resonances in hydroxamic and nitrosohydroxylamine anions.

The Cat annotation is assigned to heavy atoms with an excess of positive charge, including excess charge due to resonances (of certain groups). Generally, speaking an atom with a formal positive charge not adjacent to an atom with a formal negative charge will be assigned a Cat annotation. Care is taken to sum the charges in charge-separated ylides. NCN+ resonances are taken into account with both nitrogen atoms marked with a Cat annotation; for example, amidinium, imidazolium, 4-amino pyridine and all 3 nitrogens in guanidinium.

The O2 annotation centroid is placed between certain pairs of 1−3 Ani {S,O,N} atoms and is intended to denote an anionic −CO2 bioisostere. An O2 centroid is placed between each pair of Ani oxygens, sulfurs and anionic divalent nitrogens (in any combination). Thus, carboxylate is given one O2 centroid while −PO3 is given three O2 centroids (one between each pair of terminal Ani annotations). An exception is the tetrazole anion in which a single O2 annotation is placed at the centroid of the four nitrogen atoms in the aromatic ring.

The CN2 annotation centroid is placed on the carbon atom of NCN+ resonance structures and is intended to denote a cationic −CN2 bioisostere. The carbon between the nitrogens in amidinium, guanidinium, 2-amino pyridinium, etc. are marked with CN2 annotations.

The pi annotation applies to the Ani and Cat annotations; that is, if the annotated chemical group is a π group then the pi annotation will be added to the Ani or Cat annotations. For example, amidinium nitrogens are annotated with "Cat&pi".

Aromatic and π-Rings

The following annotations are used for aromatic and π-system rings:

  1. Aro annotation centroids are used for aromatic and pseudo aromatic rings;
  2. PiR annotation centroids are used for other π-system rings;
  3. PiN annotation centroid projections are used along the π-system plane normal;
  4. Aro2 annotation centroid normals are used do annotate the direction of the normal to the π system of Aro rings; (obsolete)
  5. HydP annotation centroids are used for planar hydrophobic rings. (obsolete)

The Aro annotation centroid is placed at the centroid of each aromatic ring (e.g. two centroids in naphthalene). The definition of aromaticity depends on the particular scheme:

  • Unified The definition of aromaticity is generous (a Daylight-style definition) in which each ring is treated in isolation and a Hueckel 4n+2 rule is applied. C=O carbons count 0 electrons, otherwise C=R count as 1; N=X nitrogens count 1 and >N− nitrogens and −O− oxygens count 2 electrons. Thus, the following are treated as (pseudo) aromatic and are given an Aro annotation centroid in the center of the ring:

  • PCH, PCHD, PCH_All, CHD. The definition of aromaticity is strict - exocyclic double bonds destroy aromatic properties of the ring. A Hueckel 4n+2 rule is applied and each π system is treated in a fully consistent manner (no isolated tests). In the above examples, only the left-most two rings are considered aromatic and given an Aro centroid annotation at center of the ring.
  • PPCH, PCHD_All. There is no definition of aromaticity in these schemes. The HydP centroid is used instead indicating a planar hydrophobic ring.

In the PPCH and PPCHD_All schemes, a HydP annotation centroid will be used instead of the PiR centroid. In the other schemes, a HydP annotation (planar hydrophobic) centroid will be placed at the center of a π ring if the majority of the ring atoms are considered hydrophobic.

Hydrophobes

The hydrophobic annotations of the various schemes differ markedly. Names and concepts are used on a per-scheme basis. Consequently, we will describe the hydrophobic treatments separately:

  1. Unified annotates hydrophobic atoms with HydA and hydrophobic centroids with Hyd.
  2. PPCH only uses HydP for planar hydrophobic centroids and HydS for non-planar hydrophobic centroids (no atoms).
  3. PPCH_All uses HydP for planar hydrophobic atoms and HydS for non-planar hydrophobic atoms (no centroids).
  4. PCH, PCHD and CHD use HydA for all hydrophobic centroids (no atoms).
  5. PCH_all uses HydA for all hydrophobic atoms (no centroids).

All of the schemes decide on hydrophobic annotations using the molecular graph topology alone - no 3D coordinates are used in the designation. Thus, hydrophobic groups are determined by graph theoretic algorithms.

The fundamental rules for deciding whether an atom is hydrophobic are encoded in the SVL function ph4_aHydrophobe. A summary of these rules is:

  1. Nitro nitrogen atoms (not in nitrate anions) are hydrophobic.
  2. Divalent sulfur atoms with two heavy neighbors bonded only to carbon or sulfur are hydrophobic.
  3. Halogens are hydrophobic.
  4. Carbon atoms are hydrophobic except a) aliphatic carbons π bonded to non-carbon atoms; or b) π carbon atoms adjacent to univalent oxygen; or c) aromatic carbon adjacent to aromatic oxygen in 5-rings; or d) carbon atoms adjacent to two or more {N,O} atoms; or e) anionic carbons in c1cccc1;

The assignment of hydrophobic annotations proceeds by first applying the preceding hydrophobic atom typing rules but leaving out fluorine atoms on the grounds that they are small and should not affect annotation placement.

The Unified scheme provides an atom-centered hydrophobic annotation, HydA and a centroid hydrophobic feature Hyd. The HydA annotation is used for hydrophobic atoms that are deemed to have sufficiently high (potential) exposure to a potential receptor. This means that, for example, sp3 carbons with 4 heavy neighbors are not marked (since they are buried) and aromatic carbons with two heavy neighbors and two ortho substituents are not annotated. The Hyd annotations are assigned by a procedure that groups connected hydrophobic atoms and assigns centroids weighted by an estimate of the likely exposed surface area of each hydrophobic atom; that is, the Hyd centroid will be placed closer to more exposed hydrophobic atoms in a hydrophobic group.

The following grouping algorithm is used:
  1. Strip Fluorines. Remove all fluorines that are not bonded to {H,F} from further consideration. Note that estimates of exposed surface area use a fluorine suppressed molecule.
  2. Rings. Find all 5-, 6-, 7-, and 8-member rings that are not composed of smaller rings. For each such ring, extract each contiguous stretch of hydrophobic atoms with at least three atoms that have a sufficiently high total exposed surface area, and generate a surface area weighted centroid annotation. Remove all annotated ring atoms from further consideration.
  3. Components. Find all connected components (single-linkage clusters) among the remaining hydrophobes and remove from consideration those clusters with an exposed surface area sum small deemed too small (less than a -CH2- group).
  4. Small Components. For the remaining hydrophobic components with three or fewer atoms, generate an exposed surface area weighted centroid annotation.
  5. Large Components. Identify the center of the component graph and generate a weighted centroid at the center including its neighbors. Remove the annotated atoms, splitting the component and apply the Small Components step and/or the Large Components step (recursively) to group the hydrophobes.

For the PCH_All scheme, all hydrophobic atoms are given a Hyd annotation. For the PPCH_All scheme, all hydrophobic atoms are given a HydP annotation if they are planar or in a π system and HydS otherwise. In either of the preceding two schemes, no centroids are used. The PCH, PCHD, CHD and PPCH schemes use only hydrophobic centroids to annotate hydrophobic regions. Some of the centroids may involve a single atom (e.g. a terminal methyl group); however, such cases result from the output of partitioning/clustering algorithm used to determine the hydrophobic centroids, all of which are marked with Hyd annotations (HydS or HydP in the case of PPCH).

The following grouping algorithm is used:
  1. Strip Fluorines. Remove all fluorines that are not bonded to {H,F} from further consideration.
  2. Rings. Find all 3-, 4-, 5-, 6-, 7-, and 8-member rings that are not composed of smaller rings and contain at least 60% hydrophobes. Generate an annotation point at the center of each such ring and remove the annotated ring atoms from further consideration.
  3. Terminals. Mark all remaining input atoms bonded to at least two other remaining input atoms. Generate an annotation point for each terminal input atom that is bonded to a marked atom and remove the terminal atom from further consideration.
  4. Components. Find all connected components (single-linkage clusters) among the remaining input atoms and generate an annotation point at the centroid of each component.

The PPCH uses a modified version of the above partition method which will assign HydP for planar groups and HydS for non-planar groups.

Link (Scaffold Replacement)

Special Link pharmacophore query features are used for scaffold replacement.

It is possible to use the Link and projected Link features manually via the Pharmacophore Query Editor to design a scaffold replacement query, however a new Scaffold Replacement application was integrated in the 2009 version of MOE that was designed to simplify the process of defining the scaffold and R-groups and generating the new optimised molecules. The Link scheme is used internally by the new Scaffold Replacement application.

Link annotations denote points of substitution on a candidate scaffold molecule as well as the locations of potential R-group substituents.

There are three types of link annotation:

  1. Link annotates a heavy atom substitution point with at least one (implicit) hydrogen.
  2. Link2 annotates projected locations of potential sp2 R-groups.
  3. Link3 annotates projected locations of potential sp3 R-groups.

Both the heavy atom substitution point and the possible R-group location(s) are given annotation points. The idea is to provide functionality similar to Bartlett's CAVEAT methodology [Lauri 1994]. The yellow annotation spheres on the heavy atoms represent substitution points and the annotations projected from the heavy atoms denote the locations of potential R-group heavy atoms.

A typical query will consist of (Link,Link2) or (Link,Link3) pairs located at positions intended to represent scaffold / R-group bonds, as illustrated in the example below. The Link2 or Link3 feature is placed on an existing R-group heavy atom and the Link feature is intended to match a heavy atom of a new scaffold to be found in a database of scaffold or linker molecules. A matching scaffold is more likely to present the existing R-group in the proper direction.

Link3 query features are used when the R-group atom is sp3. Link2 query features are used when the R-group atom is sp2. For example, when attempting to match a piperazine scaffold with an aromatic R-group, a Link2 query feature should be used so that the piperazine nitrogen conjugated planar geometry is used (and not the nitrogen tetrahedral geometry).

We now describe the annotations used to annotate scaffolds. Only {C,N,O,S,P} atoms with 1, 2 or 3 heavy neighbors and at least one (possibly implicit) hydrogen are candidates for Link features; other atoms are not considered as candidates. The following rules assume that the foregoing conditions are satisfied. Not all such atoms will be given Link annotations: substituents on freely rotatable single bonds will be avoided (e.g. sp2-sp3 rotors).

Q = any 1°, Xi = any Q = 1° {C,N+}, Y = 2° {O,S,N-} N = 2° or 3°, Q1 = 4-coord, Q2 = any
Y ≠ 1° Q = {OH, SH, NH2, PH2}   Q = {C,N,P,S}, Q = 3° sp3, Ri ≠ H
Q = 2° aromatic Q = 2° sp2 Q = 1° sp2
N = 2° amide not in 3,4-ring N = 1° amide not in 3,4-ring N = 2° in NCN+ not in 3,4-ring
N = 1° in NCN+ not in 3,4-ring O = 1° OH on Caro, Xi = 2°
   
  Q = 2° {C,N} sp3, Xi ≠ H  

Note that in most cases, Link2 and Link3 annotations are used for the projected annotations. For 2° nitrogen and carbon atoms, there is a distinction between Link2 and Link3 - Link2 projections are in the potential π system plane and Link3 projections are in tetrahedral formation. Also, note that cis peptide formation is avoided.

Solvent Exposure Filters

Projected annotations such as Don2, Acc2, ML2, Link2 and Link3 are located at potential heavy atom positions. For example, Don2 indicates a potential hydrogen bond partner heavy atoms. Realistically, this partner atom cannot have too much overlap with any of the atoms of the molecule generating the projected annotation. This condition depends on the particular conformation of a molecule and cannot be reliably predicted by topological means. Consequently, a solvent exposure test must be applied to validate any hits resulting from the Pharmacophore Search.

If a conformation hits a query, the Pharmacophore Search will apply a scheme-dependent test to verify that applicable projected features are not covered by other parts of the conformation (that would prevent the putative projection atom from occupying the intended position). The nature of the test depends on the particular scheme, but in general, it will ensure that no projected feature lies inside the hit conformation. For example, the PCH does not use projected features, so no test is applied whereas the PCHD does use projected features, but the exposure rules are different for Aro2 and Don2 projections.

Note: The Pharmacophore Query Editor displays all projected annotation points, regardless their solvent exposure. However, it does indicate the solvent exposure status of the displayed annotation points by their rendering mode: the annotation features that pass the solvent exposure test (and that will be considered by the Pharmacophore Search) are rendered as small-sphere. The annotation features that do not pass the test (and that will be ignored by the Pharmacophore Search) are rendered as point.

Custom Annotation Schemes

Each of pharmacophore annotation schemes is an SVL function that accepts a collection of atoms and generates a collection of annotation points. The Pharmacophore Applications do not contain any hard-coded assumptions about the nature of the annotation points; consequently, SVL programmers can freely create custom annotation schemes. There are several ways to customize or modify the annotation schemes:

  1. Modify the Atom Typing Functions. All of the schemes rely, in one way or another, on specific SVL functions that detect hydrogen bond donors/acceptors, cation/anions, hydrophobes, etc. These functions are described in Pharmacophore Typing Functions and their source code is in $MOE/lib/svl/ph4.svl/ph4type.svl. By modifying these functions all of the schemes can be globally customized (say, to allow nitro oxygen as a hydrogen bond acceptor). Warning! A change to the pharmacophore type functions will affect all of the schemes and some molecular descriptors. Note! Some schemes use private collections of rules for atom typing.
  2. Modify an Existing Scheme. Some schemes have annotations that do not rely on the pharmacophore atom typing functions. For example, Link annotations are encoded directly in the source code for the Unified scheme. In these cases, the scheme source code must be modified in order to customize the scheme. The source code for the standard schemes is located in $MOE/lib/svl/ph4.svl in files that start with ph4s_. By modifying the scheme files directly, hydrophobe assignment or annotations unique to the scheme can be customized. Warning! A change to the standard schemes may result in non-standard or unintended behavior.
  3. Develop a New Scheme. This is the most involved of the options. A correctly implemented scheme must provide a number of functions that will be used by the Pharmacophore Applications. These functions must conform to the conventions used by the other Pharmacophore Applications. It is good practice to start with the source code for an existing scheme, such as Unified in $MOE/lib/svl/ph4.svl/ph4s_unified.svl, change its name and then change its code. In this way, the standard behaviors can be preserved.
