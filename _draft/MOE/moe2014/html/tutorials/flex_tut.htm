The Flexible Alignment application aligns small molecules by maximizing steric and feature overlap while exploring alternative conformations with low ligand strain. The similarity score used in Flexible Alignment uses some or all the following qualitative notions of a good alignment:

  • The strain energy of each molecule is small.

  • Molecules have a similar shape.

  • Aligned atoms have similar LogP (octanol/water) values.

  • Aligned atoms have similar molar refractivities.

  • Aromatic atoms overlap.

  • Hydrogen bond donors and acceptors overlap.

  • Acidic groups and basic groups overlap.

  • Atoms of similar partial charge overlap.

  • Hydrophobic areas overlap.

To learn more about this application, see Flexible Alignment.

This tutorial is divided into three exercises:

  1. Alignment of Physostigmine and Neostigmine.

  2. Alignment of two Kinase Ligands, comparing them with the alignment with the poses when complexed with CDK2.

  3. Alignment against a Fixed Reference Structure.

Alignment of Physostigmine and Neostigmine

Physostigmine is an alkaloid extracted from the Calabar bean - a "dark brown highly poisonous seed of a tropical African woody vine, which has been used as an ordeal poison in native witchcraft trials" (Merriam Webster's Collegiate Dictionary, 10th Edition). Neostigmine binds to acetylcholinesterase in such a manner that its trimethylammonium group is at the anionic site of the enzyme. It is used in the treatment of glaucoma.

Neostigmine

  1. Open the files that contain the Physostigmine and Neostigmine molecules:

    MOE | RHS | Open

    In the Open panel, select $MOE/sample/mol/neost.moe. Hold down the Ctrl key and select $MOE/sample/mol/physos.moe. Press OK.


  2. Center the molecules in the window:

    MOE | RHS | Center

  3. If you wish, move one or both molecules to get a better view of their structural features.

  4. Choose an appropriate forcefield potential for minimization. The current forcefield setting is shown to the left of the MOE Footer:

    To change the forcefield, press the button showing the forcefield name in the MOE Footer.

    If the forcefield type is not Amber12EHT, click Load… and select Amber12EHT from the pulldown menu.

  5. Click Fix Charges to calculate partial charges for the molecules. In this example, the hydrogens are already correct, so the Fix Hydrogens button is grayed out.

    Close the Potential Setup panel.

  6. You are now ready to start the calculation. Open the Flexible Alignment panel:

    MOE | Compute | Flexible Alignment

    Change the Output Database to physos.mdb, and set the Iteration Limit to 20.

    Press OK to start the calculation.

  7. Aligned structures are written to the output database with these values:

    • U: The strain energy in kcal/mol.

    • F: The total mutual similarity score: (lower means more similar).

    • S: The value of the objective function, which is the sum of U and F.

    The best alignment will be the pose with lowest value of S, i.e. maximum similarity with the least amount of strain. This calculation typically takes 1-2 minutes.

    Tip!   Flexible Alignment uses a stochastic algorithm, so the number and precise details of the alignments may vary.

  8. Render the atoms in stick mode:

    MOE | Footer | Atoms

  9. Examine the structures in the MOE Window, as shown here:

    The carbamate groups of Physostigmine and Neostigmine align well, as do the two phenyl groups.

Alignment of two Kinase ligands

Display the CDK Ligands Complexed with CDK2

MOE includes a Kinase Database with experimental structures for protein kinases. Here, we will compare the conformations of the above inhibitors, when they are complexed with human CDK2.

  1. Open the Kinase Database Search panel:

    MOE | Protein | Search | Kinase

  2. Enter the PDB codes 1dm2 1e9h in the Text Field and click Or. This will match structures that match either one of the specified PDB codes.

  3. Click Search, select the chains 1DM2.A and 1E9H.A in the results list, click Load into MOE… and OK in the Load into MOE panel that appears.

  4. Restrict the view to the ligand binding site and hide the protein backbone ribbon:

    MOE | RHS | SiteView

    MOE | Footer | Ribbon | ×

    Note that some of the protein sidechains are aligned quite well, but some residues are displaced between the two kinase structures.

  5. Click SEQ in the top right corner of the MOE window to open the Sequence Editor. The two protein chains are at the top of the Sequence Editor window, followed by the two chains for the ligands. Reorder the chains by the chain tag and color the atoms by the chain tag using:

    SE | Edit | Order Chains | By Tag

    SE | Footer | Atoms | Tag

  6. The structures are superposed to a generic kinase alignment. The superposition can be improved by aligning just these chains:

    SE | Alignment | Align

    Press OK on the Protein Align panel.

    Note that the lactam groups in each of the ligands form a pair of hydrogen bonds with the protein backbone of the hinge region of the kinase. Note also that the sulfonate group of 1E9H occupies roughly the same space as the oxo-pyrazole ring of 1DM2.

Flexible Alignment of CDK Ligands

Having examined the aligned protein-ligand complexes, we now proceed to align the molecules using Flexible Alignment.

  1. Open the file with the unaligned ligands in the Database Viewer:

    MOE | RHS | Open and choose $MOE/sample/mol/flexcdk.mdb.

    Select both rows:

    DBV | Edit | Select All | Entries

    Load the molecules into MOE. Right-click on either cell in the mol column and:

    DBV | Molecule Cell Popup | Send Selected to MOE

    If there are already molecules in the MOE system, click Yes at the prompt, to delete them.

    Tip!   To run Flexible Alignment successfully, the molecules must have the correct number of hydrogens. In this case, hydrogens have already been added to the randomized ligands.

  2. Open the Flexible Alignment panel:

    MOE | Compute | Flexible Alignment

    Change the name of the Output Database to 1dm2_1e9h.mdb and press OK to run the calculation. The calculation will take approximately 1-2 minutes.

  3. The aligned molecules are written to the database. The U column contains the average internal energy of the ligands. The F column contains the similarity score (the lower the better) of the alignment. The S column has the sum of the U and F values. When an alignment is found with a new lowest S score, old alignments will be deleted if their S score is not within the cutoff value of the minimum S score for the new best alignment.

    Inspect the alignments as the calculation proceeds. Left-click on a cell in the mol column and drag the mouse towards lower right to enlarge the cell. Click the middle mouse button to rotate the molecules in the cell.

  4. After the calculation has finished, the best solution (the alignment with the lowest S score) will be loaded into the MOE Window. Render the molecules as sticks, with the Carbon atoms colored by the chain color and with hidden Hydrogens:

    MOE | Footer | Atoms | Stick

    MOE | Footer | Atoms | Chain

    MOE | RHS | Hydrogens

    A typical result is shown in the image below: the lactam groups are aligned. These make hydrogen bonds to the protein backbone of the kinase hinge region in the crystal complex. The sulfonate group is near the oxo-pyrazole ring. Although the orientation of the oxo-pyrazole ring may be rotated with respect to the crystal orientation, it is not unreasonable given that Flexible Alignment does not have any information about the receptor.

Alignment against a Fixed Reference Structure

One approach to using the Flexible Alignment application is to flexibly align known inhibitors to fixed ligand conformations obtained from crystal structures. In this way, one can predict the binding mode of a new ligand class based on crystal structures of ligands from different classes. In this example, the CDK2 inhibitor LS4 shown below will be flexibly aligned to a fixed reference template of two inhibitors which we will create by aligning their X-ray complexes.

Loading the LS4 Ligand

The LS4 inhibitor can be found in the PDB file 1KE8, where it is complexed with CDK2. When the PDB file is read in, the ligand is assigned to a minor tautomer state. We will correct this with the Protomer and Tautomer Viewer.

We will also convert the LS4 molecule into a 2D conformation so that structural information from the initial LS4 pose does not bias the Flexible Alignment results.

  1. Close the MOE system:

    MOE | RHS | Close

  2. Open the 1KE8 PDB file from the sample directory:

    MOE | File | Open

    In the Open panel, select $MOE/sample/mol/1ke8.pdb. Press OK in the Load PDB File panel that opens.

  3. Focus the view on the ligand and nearby atoms:

    MOE | RHS | SiteView

    Use the System panel to delete the Receptor and Solvent atoms:

    MOE | RHS | System

    Tip!   If you hold the Shift key and delete atoms from the System panel, the atoms will be deleted without a confirmation prompt.

  4. Open the Protomer and Tautomer Viewer:

    MOE | Compute | Prepare | Protomers

    The current tautomer has a lighter background. Click on the tautomer with the structure as below and click Yes to replace the atoms with the highlighted tautomer:

  5. Open the 2D Molecules panel to convert the ligand coordinates to 2D:

    MOE | RHS | Ligand | 2D Molecules

    Click the Copy to MOE button and click OK to replace the conformation in the MOE window.

  6. Render the atoms in stick mode:

    MOE | Footer | Atoms

  7. Save the LS4 ligand to a MOE file called ls4.moe.

    MOE | File | Save

    Enter a name ls4.moe and click OK.

  8. Close the MOE system:

    MOE | RHS | Close

Aligning the CDK2/Ligand Complexes

To obtain a fixed template of bound ligands for flexibly aligning LS4, we will align the protein chains to align the PDB entries 1GIH and 1G5S.

  1. Open the 1G5S and 1GIH PDB files from the sample directory:

    MOE | File | Open

    In the Open panel hold the Control key and select $MOE/sample/mol/1g5s.pdb and $MOE/sample/mol/1gih.pdb. Press OK in the Load PDB File panel that opens.


  2. Align and superpose the protein chains:

    MOE | Protein | Align

    Press OK.

  3. Delete the receptor and solvent atoms:

    MOE | Select | Receptor

    MOE | Select | Solvent

    MOE | RHS | Delete, or press the Delete key on your keyboard.

    Add hydrogens:

    MOE | Edit | Build | Hydrogens | Add Hydrogens

  4. Center the view:

    MOE | RHS | SiteView

    Color the ligand Carbon atoms by the chain color:

    MOE | Footer | Atoms | Color | Chain

  5. Fix all the atoms from the reference molecules:

    MOE | RHS | Select | All

    MOE | RHS | Constrain | Fix

    The aligned ligands are now fixed and will not move during the Flexible Alignment run.

  6. Left-click in empty space in the MOE window to clear the atom selection. The aligned CDK2 ligands should appear as shown.

Flexible Alignment of LS4 to Fixed CDK2 Ligands

We will now flexibly align the LS4 ligand to the fixed template of CDK2 ligands

  1. Load the previously prepared LS4 molecule:

    MOE | File | Open

    In the Open panel, select ls4.moe and press OK.

  2. Open the Flexible Alignment panel:

    MOE | Compute | Flexible Alignment

    Change the Output Database name if desired. Use the default settings for the other parameters. Press OK to run the calculation. The calculation typically takes between 5 and 20 minutes to complete.

  3. As the calculation proceeds, the aligned molecules are written to the database. Note that the LS4 ligand is flexibly aligned to the fixed template of the 1GIH and 1G5S ligands. The U column contains the average internal energy of the ligands. The template ligands are fixed, so variations in U are due to strain energy changes in the LS4 ligand. The F column contains the similarity score of the alignment. The column S is the sum of U and F.

  4. After the calculation completes, the solution with the lowest combined score (S) will be will be loaded into MOE.

Analyzing LS4 Flexible Alignment Results

At the end of the Flexible Alignment run, the output database is sorted by S in ascending order, i.e. the conformation with the lowest S will be at the top of the database. In many cases, alignments with low S values will be among the best alignments. However, care must be taken when examining the flexible alignment results; since the application works by balancing intramolecular strain forces with intermolecular pharmacophoric forces, pharmacophoric forces can sometimes overpower the internal strain forces in a system, yielding alignments with large similarity scores (large negative F) but which contain unreasonably strained ligands (large dU). Thus, it is always advisable to inspect the Flexible Alignment results and discard alignments with unreasonable ligand geometries.

Since the two template ligands are fixed in this example, all of the reported strain energy differences (dU) arise from the LS4 molecule. The best-scoring alignment contains a highly strained conformer of LS4, with an intramolecular H-bond and a slight out-of-place bend for the phenyl substituents. Although this alignment has a large negative S, the conformation of the LS4 ligand is unrealistic, and therefore it is probably not a good alignment.

To examine the alignments with the least strain for LS4, perform the following:

  1. Sort the database by ascending dU:

    DBV | Compute | Sort

    Set the first drop down menu to dU, click OK.

  2. The results should contain a number of alignments with dU<1 kcal/mol. To browse through the alignment results,

    Close the system:

    MOE | RHS | Close

  3. Open the Database Browser:

    DBV | File | Browse

    Center the molecules in the window:

    MOE | RHS | Center

  4. Hide the hydrogens to get a better view of the aligned ligands

    MOE | RHS | Hydrogens

  5. Turn off the display of non-bonded contacts:

    MOE | Footer | Contacts

    Deselect the H-bond checkbox in the top left corner of the panel.

  6. Change the rendering of the molecular system to stick mode and color the Carbon atoms by the chain color:

    MOE | Footer | Atoms | Stick

    MOE | Footer | Atoms | Color | Chain

  7. Visually inspect all the low-strain energy solutions up to dU=1.

  8. The low-strain solutions of LS4 should divide into two distinct alignment modes: mode I and mode II, shown in cyan in each of the two images below. The two modes are approximately 180 degree flips of one another.

    Mode I

    Mode II

  9. Both the mode I and mode II orientations of LS4 appear to be reasonable alignments with reasonable LS4 conformations. To choose the best alignment mode between these two possibilities, one could examine binding affinities for LS4 analogs and choose the alignment mode that best explains the SAR. Since X-ray data for the CDK2 target is available, one could also examine how well the different LS4 alignments fit into the CDK2 binding pocket.

LS4 Flexible Alignment Results in 1G5S pocket

To examine the LS4 alignment modes in the CDK2 binding pocket, we will open up the 1G5S structure, draw a surface around the binding pocket and browse through the Flexible Alignment results with the binding pocket surface present.

  1. Close the structures on the screen (but keep the Database Browser panel open):

    MOE | RHS | Close

    Press OK to confirm.

  2. Open the 1G5S crystal structure:

    MOE | RHS | Open

    Select $MOE/sample/mol/1g5s.pdb and press OK. Leave the default settings in the Load PDB File panel and press OK.

  3. Center the view:

    MOE | RHS | SiteView

  4. Draw a surface around the binding pocket:

    MOE | RHS | Surface | Receptor

  5. Adjust the transparency of the pocket surface from the Surfaces and Maps panel:

    MOE | RHS | Surface | Surfaces and Maps

    Select the Quick Surface in the list and adjust the transparency of the front and back faces with the slider controls:

  6. Hide the atoms just read in from the PDB file:

    MOE | RHS | Hide | All Atoms

  7. Use the Database Browser: from the database with the alignment results to browse through the alignments in the presence of the binding pocket.

  8. Change the rendering of the browsed ligands to stick mode and color the Carbon atoms by the chain color:

    MOE | Footer | Atoms | Stick

    MOE | Footer | Atoms | Color | Chain

    Now when browsing, the 1G5S ligand will now be pink, the 1GIH ligand will be brown and the LS4 will be green.

  9. As you browse through the low-dU Flexible Alignment results, note that the LS4 ligand penetrates through the binding pocket in many of the mode II alignments. In contrast, most of the mode I alignments have LS4 poses that do not penetrate the binding pocket.

    Mode I

    Mode II

  10. Browse through the alignments and pick the mode I alignment with the lowest dU that does not penetrate the binding pocket.

  11. Press Keep in the Database Browser to keep the alignment. Then Close the browser.

  12. We now have a low-strain alignment of LS4 that does not collide with the CDK2 binding pocket. This is the best predicted alignment, based on this procedure.

    MOE | RHS | SiteView

  4. Use the Database Browser from the database with the alignment results:

    DBV | File | Browse

  5. Hide all the atoms:

    MOE | RHS | Hide | All Atoms

  6. Display the crystal ligand and the aligned ligand using the System panel:

    MOE | RHS | System

  7. Press the icon on the 1KE8 row and press Ligand 1KE8.A to display the crystal structure. Press on the (no name) row to display the aligned ligand.
