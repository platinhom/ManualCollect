Overview

The Ligand Interactions application provides a means to visualize an active site of a complex in diagrammatic form. The content is presented in a concise and stylized manner, and summarizes a large amount of spatial data making use of mnemonic glyphs where appropriate. The panel can be used as a complement to 3D visualization, as the 2D and 3D representations can be displayed alongside one another. The content can also be exported in graphical or tabular form, as well as printed.

The diagram itself consists of the selected ligand as the centerpiece, which is drawn using the traditional schematic style for molecules. A selection of interacting entities, which includes hydrogen-bonded residues, close but non-bonded residues, solvent molecules and ions are drawn about the ligand, their positions in 2D being chosen to be representative of the observed 3D distances, as well as taking into account aesthetic considerations. Additional properties such as solvent accessible surface area and the ligand proximity outline are also shown.

The user interface provides control over whether certain elements are drawn, and to some extent their color and style. The definition of hydrogen bonded, ionic and proximity interactions can be affected by modifying cutoff values. The interactions which are shown in the 2D diagram in the panel are reflected also in the MOE main window.

Methodology

The information content displayed in the Ligand Interactions panel is similar to that presented by the popular program LIGPLOT [Wallace 1995], except with a stronger emphasis on aesthetics. A full treatment of the method is described in the literature [Clark 2007].

Generation of the protein-ligand annotation diagram consists of the following steps:

  1. Ascertain the interactions between the ligand and the species immediately surrounding it, and decide which residues, solvents and ions are to be included in the diagram.

  2. Layout the coordinates of the ligand, taking into consideration the extent to which the flattened version is comparable to the 3D conformation.

  3. Position the residues with strong hydrogen bonds to the ligand, arranging them in the plane and reproducing the 3D distances in 2D as best as possible.

  4. Position the residues which are proximal to the ligand but do not have strong contacts, preserving 3D distances to the extent possible.

  5. Calculate the proximity contour and solvent accessibility.

  6. Draw each of the entities.

Calculate Interactions

The atoms which constitute the ligand must first be decided upon. By default, this is the set of atoms which would be selected using MOE | Select | Ligand, further reduced by including only the largest continuously connected component. Other definitions of the ligand may be used, but the set must indicate a continuously connected molecular species.

Hydrogen Bonds: Each pair of heavy atoms from the ligand set and the receptor set could be a hydrogen bond if: (1) there is a tautomer combination in which one of the atoms is a potential H-bond donor and the other is a potential H-bond acceptor, and (2) the distance and orientation are favorable for the particular functional group. For each viable ligand:receptor atom pair an interaction energy is calculated, and it is possible to set the minimum bound for what is considered to be hydrogen bond. The directionality of the hydrogen bond is also noted; it is possible for a ligand:receptor pair to be potentially both a donor and an acceptor.

Solvent Interactions: Ligand atoms can form hydrogen bonds with solvent molecules, typically water. The perception and scoring is done in the same way as for ligand:receptor pairs, except that solvent hydrogen bonds are only retained if the solvent molecule has a qualifying hydrogen bond to the ligand. If the solvent molecule has additionally a hydrogen bond to the receptor it is drawn as well. Un-anchored solvent molecules are considered to be fluxional and transient, and do not appear in the diagram.

Metal Ligation: Metal atoms which are bonded to the ligand, or are close enough to be within bonded distance, are classified as ligand:metal ligation interactions. As with hydrogen bonds, energies for the metal ligation interactions are calculated, which may be used as a minimum bound for inclusion.

Nonbonded Residues: Residues which approach closely to the ligand but do not have any qualifying strong interactions (i.e. hydrogen bonds) may be classified as non-bonded residues that have a significant effect on the orientation and binding of the ligand, but which may be spread out over a number of pairwise contacts, each of which is relatively weak. Classification of nonbonded residues is done by the following simple formula:

cutoff = parameter (default 4.5 Å)
num = number of heavy atoms in the residue closer than cutoff
limit = cutoff - 0.9 + 0.1 * num

The residue must have at least one atom which approaches the ligand at a distance less than limit to qualify as having a nonbonded interaction. In this way, residues with few atoms which are particularly close, or many atoms which are quite close, are selected for inclusion in the diagram. They are not however assigned any kind of score, and are arranged much less rigorously than those with a specific strong interaction.

Flattening Depiction

The focal point of the 2D diagram produced by the Ligand Interactions tool is the ligand itself, which is arranged prior to placement of the residues with which it interacts. In order to produce an aesthetic layout, the MOE depiction layout algorithm is invoked [Clark 2006]. If called without modification, however, the standard depiction method would produce a 2D layout that maximizes dispersion between non-bonded atoms, within its set of constraints. This is not always an ideal starting point, since there is often a viable solution which does not maximize dispersion, but more closely reflects the original 3D conformation.

In order to encourage a 2D layout solution which suits the needs of the Ligand Interactions diagram, the layout engine is provided with a callback function which offsets the scores of each proposed layout with its own criteria. The modifications are crafted so that they are of sufficient magnitude to overwhelm the depiction layout algorithm's inverse-distance-squared repulsions between nonbonded atoms, but not large enough to force the depictor prematurely into its damage-control methods.

The score for each proposed depiction layout is modified like so:

score = score + Σtorsion w |θ3D - θ2D| + Σi,j w |dist3Dij - dist2Dij|

The list of torsion quadruplets, and the original 3D angles, are determined before the process begins. In each case the 2D layout is penalized according to its relative deviation from the proper conformation, which rewards choices that resemble the original geometry.

Similarly, the distance matrix of heavy atom pairs [i,j] where i < j is formed, and scaled such that the average bond length in 3D is the same as the 2D depiction ideal. The 2D layout is penalized less if the distances are similar to the 3D conformation. The weighting for this term is set to the inverse square of the 3D distance, in order to prevent long range terms from overly dominating the score. For pairs where one of the atoms has a strong contact to a residue, the weighting is 10-times heavier, and those for which both atoms have a strong contact are magnified 100-fold.

It is important to note that the resulting depiction is not at all guaranteed to resemble the 3D conformation, but rather it is encouraged to do so, as long as primary aesthetic constraints can still be satisfied. Highly non-planar molecules (e.g. the orthogonal conformations of biphenyl derivatives) require compromise. The techniques which are described subsequently desire but do not require faithful correspondence with the 2D ligand coordinates and the proper original conformation.

Position Ligated Metals

Metals which are classified as being ligated to the ligand are temporarily promoted to being a part of the ligand, immediately prior to the layout described above, then subsequently disconnected. This results in the position of the metal being obtained from the depiction layout.

Position Hydrogen-bonded Residues

Residues and solvent molecules which are bound to the ligand by a hydrogen bond are placed next. There are many competing effects which determine where a strongly interacting residue is positioned. Since a line will be drawn on the final diagram for each such interaction, it is very important to try to find a position in which the 2D distances of these interactions proportionally reflects the 3D distance. But it is also important to provide an impression of the spatial relationship to other nearby residues, and to place them in such a way that they do not obscure other features such as bonds, atoms or other residues.

Initial placement: Finding an initial guess position for each residue is very important, because the main placement criteria are expressed as a continuous multivariable optimization problem. Any starting point which is in a far-from-optimal local minimum must be avoided. As a general consideration, starting points should be far from atoms and bonds, and not inside rings, nor inside "pincer"-like formations.

In order to ensure that the basic requirements are met, a coarse grid is placed around the ligand, and the repulsions from specific regions are overlayed, as is shown in the following diagram:

1. Base avoidance 2. Specific attraction

The base repulsive grid (1) is formed by adding Gaussian distributions at atom and ring centers, which ensure that the minimum point will be well outside of the molecular region. The grid in (2) shows the placement guide for a residue that has a single strong contact to the nitrogen atom which is highlighted with a circle. The overlay function is [1 - d / max d], where d = distance between grid point and contacting atom. The grid point with the lowest value is shown by the X mark, and serves as the starting position for the residue.

Multivariable Refinement: Each residue atom which has a strong contact to the ligand is placed in the method described above. Then, each of the (x,y) coordinates is defined as a variable, and they are collectively refined by minimization of the following continuously differentiable energy function:

energy = Σij wij (dist2ij - D2)2       (variable-variable desired distances)
+ Σij wij exp [-K dist2ij]       (variable-variable short range repulsion)
+ Σik wik (dist2ik - D2)2       (variable-fixed point desired distances)
+ Σik wik exp [-K dist2ik]       (variable-fixed point short range repulsion)
+ Σθ wθ exp [-½K (1 - cos θ)2]       (variable-fixed point angular repulsion)

The variable-variable terms are primarily based on the desired distance, which is that of the 3D environment of the receptor, which should be matched in 2D when possible. Since the term is distance to the power of 4, the weighting is scaled down by the 3D distance, in order to ensure that nearby residues influence each other more significantly. Short-range repulsion between variable terms are expressed as a Gaussian type function, which is scaled such that it dominates in the region of <1 Å, but fades quickly with greater distance.

Variable-fixed point desired distances are composed of the strong interactions to the ligand atoms. All ligand:receptor atom pairs which are not connected by a strong interaction are accumulated in the variable-fixed point repulsion terms.

Variable-fixed point angular repulsion terms appear when more than one receptor atom is connected to a single ligand atom, in which case the angle formed between each instance of &theta = vari-fixed-varj is highly repulsive when θ is small.

Consolidation: The atoms belonging to particular residues are collapsed into one point per residue by averaging positions. The energy function is updated accordingly with the interactions and distances merged, and the new variables are resubmitted to the energy minimization, to obtain final positions for the strongly-bound residues.

Position Non-bonded Residues

Nonbonded residues are subjected to the same initial placement algorithm described above, except that the already-placed residues are also included in the regions of the surface to avoid. The same energy minimization process is used, except that the terms are employed differently. The variable-variable desired distance terms are not used. The variable-fixed point desired distances are set to be the initial placement positions, with a very light weighting. Variable-fixed point repulsion terms include ligand atoms, as well as the positions of residues which have previously been placed, and there is no angular repulsion term.

The overall effect is that the nonbonded residues are more sensitive to the initial placement algorithm, which is relative to all of the ligand atoms within a mild proximity of each residue, but are also more flexible in terms of deviation therefrom. They tend to be spaced evenly, and take up space beyond or in between the previously placed entities.

Proximity and Surfaces

While the arrangement of ligands and residues is frequently sufficient to gain an insight into the proximity of the species in the active site, the information content can be significantly enhanced by calculating properties based on the 3D data, then presenting this data in a pictorial summary form.

Proximity Contour: It useful to be able to observe how much space is available to the more outward-facing parts of the ligand, which can be expressed in its final form as a contour line surrounding the ligand. In areas where the exterior of the ligand is close to the receptor cavity, the line will be drawn tightly around the ligand in 2D, and loosely when there is plenty of available space.

In order to calculate the data for the contour, for each atom some number of trajectories (possibly zero) are generated. The trajectories are dependent on the geometry of the atom and the number of substituents. If there are insufficient anchor points, as is the case for sp3 and sp2 atoms with only one neighbor, a sampling set is created. Otherwise, the trajectories are indicative of the positions that would typically be occupied by a hydrogen substituent:

sp3/3 sp3/2 sp3/1
sp2/2 sp2/1 sp/1

For each of the trajectories, a "cannonball" of radius 0.8 Å is fired along the line. Every other atom in the system, except the origin, is considered to be a ball of van der Waals size. The extent is the distance which the cannonball travels before intersecting with another atom, bounded below by zero and above by 1.5 Å. If multiple trajectories are assigned to the atom, the average is used.

The contour that is plotted from the resulting extents is useful as a measure of substitution space. For example, if by examining the diagram it appears that there is room for a methyl group, this is likely to be the case in the actual active site:


Proximity contour: 1RO6 Hydrolase

In the above complex, it can clearly be seen that there is a significant amount of space available for direct substitution on any of the three ring systems, especially the terminal cyclopentyl group, but that the heteroatoms and one side of the aromatic ring are more congested.

Solvent Accessibility: Two types of solvent accessibility metrics are available: ligand solvent accessibility, and residue solvent accessibility. Both of these quantities are based on measuring exposed surface area once each of the atoms has been assigned a van der Waals-like radius + 1.4 Å, the latter part assuming that the solvent is water. Qualitatively, this means that if an atom's assigned sphere is completely subsumed by the spheres of other atoms in the ensemble, its exposure is 0. If an atom exists alone in space with no overlap, its exposure is 4πr2. Deeply buried atoms tend to have almost zero exposed surface area, whereas for instance a terminal substituent on a ligand which is sticking out of the active site may have an exposed surface area on the order of half the maximum possible value.

For solvent accessibility of ligands, the surface accessibility calculation is carried out on the ligand + receptor complex, i.e. the receptor residues will also cover up the surfaces of the ligand atoms, if they are close enough. In the following example, the oligopeptide ligand is oriented "through" the active site, like an embedded arrow:


Ligand solvent accessibility: 1AAQ Hydrolase

The part of the ligand which is shown on the right hand side of the diagram projects significantly far from the active site, and two of the terminal methyl groups are very highly exposed to solvent. The opposite end of the ligand, shown on the left hand side, is also exposed, but to a lesser extent.

Solvent exposure of receptor residues is calculated by examining the difference between the solvent-exposed surface area of the receptor with and without the presence of the ligand. The differences are summed for the heavy atoms belonging to each residue. If the value is zero, then the residue exposure to solvent is not affected at all by the ligand. A high value probably means that a number of residue atoms are "covered up" by the ligand, which is often indicative of hydrophobic interactions which are collectively important. A small value likely means that just one or two of the residue atoms are moderately affected by the presence of the ligand.

In the following example:


Receptor solvent accessibility: 1AAQ Hydrolase

The receptor exposure differences are shown by the size and intensity of the turquoise discs surrounding the residues. The ArgA8 and GlyB48 residues (on the right hand side) express a side-chain and backbone segment, respectively, which are both very highly exposed to solvent in the active site when the ligand is absent. The presence of the ligand greatly reduces the solvent accessible surface area.

Drawing

The Ligand Interactions panel calculates the desired properties using the contents of the MOE molecular system for input, modified by user-specified parameters. This data is then used to prepare a 2D graphics object, in conjunction with user-specified aesthetic preferences. The graphics object is used to display the contents in the panel, and also for printing and exporting.

Concurrent Layout

Ligand Interactions operates differently when the input is specified as a series of bound complexes, rather than a single instance in isolation. Depending on the combination of options selected, the following additional circumstances apply:

  1. The flattened ligands are co-aligned on a common manifold, so their relative orientations are preserved;

  2. The residues are placed simultaneously around all of the ligands, so a single layout is found which suits all of the complexes;

  3. The diagrams are labeled with additional annotations depending on the extent to which various properties are conserved.

The following example shows a single protein, to which two docked poses of a single ligand have been selected:

The two poses are oriented in an opposite way, and this observation is reflected in the 2D diagram. The residue positions are identical in both cases. In the first diagram there is a hydrogen bond to residue B35, which is annotated with a bar behind the directional arrow to show that the interaction is not conserved throughout the series. In the second diagram, the hydrogen bond does not exist, so the interaction is shown as "dangling".

Running the Application

To use the Ligand Interactions panel, load a ligand-receptor complex into MOE and then select MOE | Compute | Ligand Interactions or MOE | RHS | Ligand | Ligand Interactions. The panel will open, displaying a 2D depiction of the ligand and its interactions with proximal residues of the protein. If the contents of the molecular system change while the panel is up, press Apply to recalculate the interaction data and update the depiction.

In the panel below, the ligand-receptor interactions for the complex found in the file $MOE/sample/mol/1RO6.moe are shown.

For most ligand/protein complexes, the default settings used to produce the diagram should be reasonable.

Receptor By default, the set of atoms with which the ligand may potentially interact is the union of the atoms which would be selected by MOE | Select | Receptor and by MOE | Select | Solvent. The receptor atoms can be changed to another set definition if necessary. Pressing the ? button shows which atoms are included in the currently indicated set.
Ligand The default ligand atoms are those which would be selected by MOE | Select | Ligand. Because the 2D diagram only includes one connected component, it is often necessary to provide more specific information if there are multiple disconnected species which are considered to be ligands. The simplest way to accomplish this is to select only the desired ligand, and change the value of the option widget to Selected Atoms. To check which atoms are included in the current set, click on the ? button.
... series Special circumstances arise when the MOE main window contains what are clearly indicated as multiple ligands, and one or more receptors. Instead of the default selection for the Receptor and Ligand fields, the content will look more like:

Whenever the ligand+receptor pairs begin with the same complex number, the diagram which is generated uses the concurrent layout method. Also, the up and down buttons which appear to the right of the input selection allow for a quick and convenient way to step through the series of complexes.

Options The Options pager controls the contents of the panel below it, which initially shows the Legend contents. Other pages present a variety of modifications which can be made to the way the ligand interaction data is calculated and presented.

Legend: A graphical key to help interpret the 2D part of the Ligand Interactions panel is given. The legend changes to reflect the currently selected aesthetic annotation preferences.

2D Contact Style: The manner in which the contacts are drawn in 2D can be controlled using this page. Hydrogen bonds between the ligand and the receptor are subdivided into bonds to the residue Backbone or the Sidechain. Interactions between the ligand and the Solvent are drawn distinctly. Ionic interactions include metals which were found to be chelated, or were bonded to the ligand in the original structure. In cases where the ligand is covalently bound to the protein, the severed bond which demarcates the link between the two is drawn using the same style policy as is used for ionic bonds.

Line style options include None, Dot, Dash and Solid, and arbitrary colors may be chosen. When the Distance box is checked, the interaction types will be annotated by the distance between the two heavy atoms, in angstroms. When the Energy box is selected, the interaction energy is shown in kcal/mol.

2D Residue Colors: Residue colors can be changed for the 2D diagram. The color of the residue specifically refers to the color with which the circle is filled, and any further annotation. By default, all types of residues are set to Auto.

The residues occurring in the diagram are one of four types, respectively: H-bonded refers to residues with hydrogen bonds to the ligand. Metal refers to metals which are close enough to be included in the diagram, though not actually bonded (very tightly bound metals are treated specially, and drawn as part of the ligand). Solvent refers to solvent molecules, usually water, hydrogen bonded to the ligand. Nonbonded refers to receptor residues which are close to but do not have a hydrogen bond to the ligand.

The possible coloring schemes for each of the residue types are Auto, User, Residue, Chain and None. The meaning of Auto depends on the residue type. For receptor residues, whether they are bonded or not, the main color is light green if the residue is "greasy" (i.e. does not have a polar or charged sidechain), or light mauve otherwise. Furthermore if the side chain is basic, the residue is annotated with a blue ring; if it is acidic, it receives a red ring. Solvent residues of type Auto are drawn with no interior fill, and metal residues are colored gray.

Types User, Residue, Chain and None are interpreted in the same way regardless of the residue type. When User is selected, a Color-chooser widget appears, allowing the specific color to be selected. Residue and Chain cause the residue to be colored according to the corresponding color of the MOE object. Note that the default color for residues is black, which is translated into transparent in this case. Selecting None makes the residue transparent, i.e. it is drawn with only a border and text.

Diagram Options: Additional annotations in the 2D diagram can be enabled or disabled.

  • Proximity Contour refers to the dotted outline that surrounds the ligand, denoting distance to the active site interior.
  • Ligand Solvent Exposure refers to the blue smudges that are drawn behind some of the ligand atoms, to denote extent of solvent exposure.
  • Receptor Solvent Exposure refers to the turquoise discs that are drawn behind some of the residues, to denote the difference in solvent exposure due to the presence of the ligand.
  • Inter-residue Contacts allows hydrogen bonds and non-peptidic covalent bonds between receptor residues to be annotated.
  • Bound Solvent Contacts causes residues which interact with non-labile solvent molecules to be included in the diagram if they were otherwise insufficiently proximal.
  • Arene System Contacts causes π-π, π-H and π-cation interactions to be annotated.
  • Union of Receptor Residues insists that for a series of complexes, any residue which qualifies for inclusion in any diagram is included in all diagrams.
  • Fixed Receptor Positions causes concurrent receptor optimization for a series; if not on, then the ligands will be co-aligned, but the receptors will arrange themselves separately for each diagram.
  • Conserved Features causes additional annotations to be added to a series to show any interactions which are only observed in some cases, as well as for residues which do not have the same amino acid type throughout the series.
  • Site Wall Clash enables checking for ligand atoms which approach receptor atoms too closely. Cases where such heavy atoms are closer than 1.2 Å less than the sum of the van der Waals distances are marked as clashing atoms. If clashing atoms disconnect the ligand into multiple components, then all but the largest component is also categorized as clashing. Clashing atoms are drawn in red, and all interactions with the receptor are invalidated.

Rendering Options: Several miscellaneous style preferences can be set within this option:

Rotation allows the diagram to be rotated by the given angle. If Reflection is checked, then the diagram is reflected across the X-axis prior to being rotated. Residue Size allows the amount of space taken up by the cartoonish residue labels to be modified from the default setting. Single Letter Residues allows the single letter residue codes to be used instead of the default 3 letter codes.

Parameters: The criteria for the various types of contacts can be controlled on this page. The H-bond, and Ionic values refer to the minimum interactions energies (in kcal/mol) required in order to qualify. Note that residues which possess a hydrogen bond or ionic metal bond to the ligand that does not meet the cutoff will most likely be reclassified as nonbonded residues, and still be represented in the diagram. Solvent residues which do not qualify for a sufficiently strong hydrogen bond generally do not feature in the diagram.

The Cutoff distance refers to the distance which a residue atom must be within in order to activate the calculation for deciding whether or not the whole residue meets the criteria, as described previously.

Export The Export panel allows the main contents of the 2D Ligand Interactions diagram to be exported to a picture format:

A format must be selected, which may be one of the supported bitmap formats (PNG, JPG, BMP). On Microsoft Windows platforms, the Clipboard option is available, which will paste a BMP representation onto the clipboard instead of writing to a file.

The filename should have an extension appropriate for the type. The width and height default to an arbitrary but reasonable scale for the picture. The slider can be used to scale isotropically, or the width and height can be entered specifically to achieve anisotropic scaling.

The content can include the Diagram and/or the Legend, which is controlled by checkboxes.

The background can be either Transparent or White. Note that JPG and BMP formats do not support transparency.

Apply To regenerate the 2D picture shown in the panel, as well as the annotations in the MOE Window, click the Apply button. The configuration options presented throughout the panel will not be updated until this button is clicked.
Isolate To render the active site in 3D, click the Isolate button, which will hide all atoms which are not close to the active site. After this operation has been performed, the 2D and 3D presentations should be readily comparable.
Print To prepare the 3D image for printing or output to a printer-like device, click the Print button, and prepare the content as necessary:

!

The right hand side of the printer setup dialog is in two parts: general print options which are common to all MOE print capabilities (top right), and configuration options specific to Ligand Interactions (bottom right).

The title is on by default, and is initially set to the description of the molecular system. Its text can be altered, as can its font, size and position. The title may contain simple HTML tags. The Apply button must be used to update the title.

A multiline footnote may be added, which will be displayed at the bottom left of the page. Its font and size may be edited, and simple HTML tags are supported. The Apply button must be used to update the footnote.

By default the picture scales to 100% of the maximum size remaining on the page, which may be changed. A thin black frame can optionally be added. The legend is not displayed by default, but it can be added by checking the Legend box.

If the current rendition is of a series of complexes, three additional options will be available: Tiling, Ligand Name and Receptor Name. The tiling affects how the aligned pictures of the series are arranged on the page(s). The other two options specify whether the chain names of the ligands and/or receptors are used as titles for the respective tiles.

To obtain a textual list of interactions to accompany the printout, toggle on the List Interactions checkbox. Note that this text may appear on a separate page from the interaction diagram.
