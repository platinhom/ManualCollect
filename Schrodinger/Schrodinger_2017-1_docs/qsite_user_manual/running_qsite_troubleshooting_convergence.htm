Convergence Problems in QSite Jobs

For difficult to converge QSite cases, the following recommendations may help:

  • Do not use non-bonded cutoffs (clear the Use non-bonded cutoffs box in the Potential tab). Using non-bonded cutoffs leads to energy jumps when the number of non-bonded interactions changes, as residues or atoms cross the cutoff boundary. This causes convergence problems, and the energies obtained are not comparable between jobs unless the number of non-bonded interactions happens to be the same. Turning off the use of cutoffs increases the time taken for the job. An alternative is to use a distance-dependent dielectric for the MM calculations.

  • Do not use loose MM convergence criteria. In the MM Minimization tab the default is an RMS gradient of 0.01, so values much larger than that would be considered loose. Even if you are running a quick job, using loose convergence criteria for the MM side is likely to be counterproductive, as the MM region will not be able to move enough to be stable with respect to the QM region and the adiabatic approximation will not then be valid.

  • Freeze the outer parts of the protein in the MM region. There are many low-frequency modes for rotation of peripheral groups on the protein that can contribute noise to the gradient. The exact orientation of these groups is usually irrelevant to the interactions in the active sites, so they can be frozen without significantly affecting the results. One strategy for freezing MM atoms is to take a buffer distance from the QM region, and freeze the residues outside this distance. To test the effect of the freezing, you can consider increasing the buffer distance and re-running the calculation.
